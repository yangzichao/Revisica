\begin{theorem}
Find all values of $x$ that satisfy the equation $x = \!\sqrt{11-2x} + 4$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Step 0: To start, let's square both sides of the equation to get rid of the square root.
Step 1: No, first let's subtract $4$ from both sides.
Step 2: Oh right, so $x-4=\sqrt{11-2x}$ and then $(x-4)^2=11-2x$.
Step 3: Now we can expand and solve for $x$.
Step 4: The left side is $x^2-8x+16$ so our equation is now $x^2-8x+16=11-2x$.
Step 5: Let's move everything to one side so that we can put this equation in standard form.
Step 6: That means we should add $2x$ to both sides.
Step 7: And subtract $11$ from both sides.
Step 8: Now we have $x^2-6x+5=0$.
Step 9: Which factors as $(x-1)(x-5)=0$.
Step 10: So either $x-1=0$ or $x-5=0$.
Step 11: That means $x$ is either $1$ or $5$.
Step 12: Right, and we should check to make sure that these solutions work.
Step 13: Plugging in $1$ we get $1=\sqrt{11-2(1)}+4$ which simplifies to $1=\sqrt{9}+4$.
Step 14: This simplification confirms that $1=7$, which proves $1$ is certainly a solution.
Step 15: Well, now we check $5$?
Step 16: Plugging in $5$ we get $5=\sqrt{11-2(5)}+4$ which simplifies to $5=\sqrt{1}+4$.
Step 17: And $5=1+4$ is true.
Step 18: That means the solutions are $x=1$ and $x=5$.
\end{proof}

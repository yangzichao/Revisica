## Executive Summary
This draft has three central problems: it argues as an advocate rather than as a researcher, it makes empirical claims without presenting evidence, and it treats a textbook competitive-equilibrium result as if it settles a large empirical literature. For an `econ-top5` target, these are not line-edit problems; they are framing, contribution, and credibility problems.

The highest-value revision is to decide what this paper is trying to be. If it is a serious economics paper, it needs a neutral research question, a real literature position, a defensible contribution, and either actual empirical analysis or an explicitly theoretical scope. If it is only a short pedagogical note, it should be retargeted to a very different venue.

## Basic Language Issues
- Sentence-level grammar error:
  > "Some people think it's good, some think it's bad."
  
  This is a comma splice. It weakens polish in a highly visible part of the introduction. Rewrite as two sentences or use a semicolon.

- Ambiguous reference:
  > "Also Card and Krueger (1994) found the opposite"
  
  "The opposite" is too vague. State the actual claim being contrasted, such as no negative employment effect or a smaller-than-expected effect.

- Vague wording:
  > "Figure~1 shows the relationship"
  
  "The relationship" does not identify which variables or quantities are being related. Name the objects explicitly.

- Imprecise source description:
  > "I looked at some data from the internet"
  
  This is too vague even at the sentence level. It does not tell the reader what data, from where, or why it is credible.

## Structure and Logic Issues
- The introduction states a conclusion before establishing a question, gap, or contribution:
  > "I personally think the evidence clearly shows it's bad, and I'll prove it here."
  
  This removes the paper’s argumentative arc. The introduction should move from question to literature to contribution to preview, not from opinion to asserted proof.

- The paper overgeneralizes from a stylized benchmark model:
  > "Now if we set a minimum wage $\bar{w} > w^*$ then clearly $S(\bar{w}) > D(\bar{w})$ which means there's unemployment. QED."
  
  The logic is valid only within a simple competitive model. The draft then treats that conditional result as decisive proof about real-world policy. That is the main claim-evidence mismatch.

- The empirical section is structurally empty:
  > "I looked at some data from the internet and it seems to confirm my model."
  
  > "Figure~1 shows the relationship but I couldn't get the plotting software to work so you'll have to take my word for it."
  
  A section titled "Empirical Evidence" needs data, design, and actual results. As written, it offers none, and explicitly asks the reader to accept unsupported claims.

- The draft does not meaningfully situate itself in the literature:
  > "A bunch of economists have argued about it forever."
  
  > "Also Card and Krueger (1994) found the opposite but their methodology is super flawed in my opinion."
  
  This is not a literature review. The paper needs substantive engagement with major theoretical and empirical work, not generic controversy framing plus dismissal.

## Scholarly Rhetoric Issues
- The abstract uses conversational, self-validating language instead of scholarly summary:
  > "So basically everyone knows that minimum wage laws mess up the labor market."
  
  > "In this paper I'm going to show you a really cool model that proves it."
  
  > "My model is way better than what other people have done before."
  
  > "Trust me, the math checks out."
  
  This language damages credibility immediately. The abstract should state the question, method, main result, and scope in neutral terms.

- The treatment of prior literature is polemical rather than analytical:
  > "their methodology is super flawed in my opinion."
  
  This is not an acceptable way to engage a canonical paper. If the draft disagrees, it must name the specific methodological issue and explain why it matters.

- The conclusion is combative and self-promotional:
  > "I've conclusively proven that minimum wage laws cause unemployment."
  
  > "Policymakers should listen to economists like me instead of pushing ideological agendas."
  
  > "This paper is definitely publishable in a top economics journal."
  
  These sentences should be removed or fully rewritten. A conclusion should summarize findings, qualify scope, and state implications carefully.

## Venue-Style Gap
- The title is incompatible with `econ-top5` norms:
  > "\title{Why Minimum Wage Laws Are Bad: A Super Quick Math Proof}"
  
  It is normative, colloquial, and pre-judges the answer. A top-field title should describe the question, mechanism, or contribution in neutral language.

- The abstract is missing the core elements expected in a top economics submission:
  > "So basically everyone knows..."
  
  It does not state a research puzzle, methodology, contribution, or limitation in a recognizable top-journal format.

- The model does not currently offer a top-journal contribution:
  > "Consider a labor market with supply $S(w) = aw$ and demand $D(w) = b - cw$"
  
  As written, this is a textbook price-floor result. For `econ-top5`, a theory paper would need a nontrivial mechanism, a puzzle, or new comparative statics.

- The manuscript lacks the citation and evidence infrastructure expected by the venue:
  > "Also Card and Krueger (1994) found the opposite..."
  
  There is no real literature review, no reference list, and no credible empirical apparatus. That is a direct venue-fit failure.

## Suggested Rewrites
- Title:
  > Current: "Why Minimum Wage Laws Are Bad: A Super Quick Math Proof"
  
  Suggested:
  > "Minimum Wage Policy and Employment in a Competitive Labor Market"

- Introduction claim:
  > Current: "I personally think the evidence clearly shows it's bad, and I'll prove it here."
  
  Suggested:
  > "This paper examines how a binding minimum wage affects employment in a competitive labor market and clarifies which conclusions follow from that benchmark framework."

- Literature sentence:
  > Current: "Also Card and Krueger (1994) found the opposite but their methodology is super flawed in my opinion."
  
  Suggested:
  > "Card and Krueger (1994) report limited disemployment effects in their study of fast-food employment; this paper differs by analyzing the implications of a competitive benchmark model and discusses the scope conditions under which the model’s predictions may or may not align with that evidence."

- Conclusion claim:
  > Current: "I've conclusively proven that minimum wage laws cause unemployment."
  
  Suggested:
  > "Under the assumptions of a competitive labor-market model, a binding minimum wage generates excess labor supply; the empirical relevance of this mechanism depends on market structure and institutional context."

## Needs Human Check
- Intended genre:
  > "A Super Quick Math Proof"
  
  If this is meant as a pedagogical note, blog-style essay, or classroom exposition, many `econ-top5` criticisms become less relevant. If it is meant as a research article, the current framing is untenable.

- Intended scope of Section 3:
  > "\section{Empirical Evidence}"
  
  > "I couldn't get the plotting software to work so you'll have to take my word for it."
  
  Decide whether the author will actually provide empirical analysis. If not, the section should likely be removed rather than repaired.

- Intended contribution level:
  > "My model is way better than what other people have done before."
  
  If the author has an unstated novel mechanism, it needs to be made explicit. If not, the paper should not claim originality on the basis of the current model.

## Revision Priorities
1. Reposition the paper: decide whether it is a real research article or a pedagogical/theoretical note for a different venue.
2. Rewrite the title, abstract, and introduction in neutral academic prose; remove advocacy, self-praise, and claims of proof.
3. Either build a real literature review and empirical section with proper citations, data, and figures, or cut the empirical claims entirely.
4. Reframe the model as a conditional benchmark result under explicit assumptions, not as a decisive resolution of the policy debate.
5. Clean up the remaining sentence-level issues: comma splice, ambiguity, vague references, and imprecise wording.
